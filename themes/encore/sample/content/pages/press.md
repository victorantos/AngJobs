## One line

The Low Bridge are a four-piece soul band from the wrong end of the river:
horn-driven, harmony-heavy, and better live — which the records already
knew.

## Fifty words

The Low Bridge write soul songs about small hours and long rivers:
four voices, a horn section's worth of argument, and a rhythm section
that learned its patience on late-night ferries. Two records in, their
shows sell out rooms the industry forgot to watch. New album **Small
Hours** out now.

## Two hundred words

The Low Bridge formed the way the best bands do: by accident, in a kitchen,
during someone else's party. Two records later the accident has horn
charts.

**Small Hours** (2026), recorded in six nights in a decommissioned chapel,
trades the debut's elbows for space — songs about night shifts, last
buses, and the specific loneliness of a town that empties at six. It
entered the independent chart at 11 with no label, no agency, and a spreadsheet
where the manager should be.

Live is the argument: reviewers keep reaching for words like "revival
meeting," and the band keep selling out the rooms nobody scouts. The
strikethroughs on their tour page have become a running joke with the
fanbase and a quiet threat to promoters who book small.

They are, in order of who answers email fastest: Ada (vocals, guitar),
Marsh (drums), Ini (bass, arrangements), and Cole (keys, horns, denial).
