*Fill in each section below, then delete these instructions. The `description` doubles as the excerpt on the archive page, so write it as a subtitle, not a summary. Set `draft: false` when the letter is ready to send.*

*Open with your strongest line.* Not a warm-up, not "I've been thinking lately" — the sentence you'd be tempted to save for the middle. Put it first and earn it for the rest of the page.

*Then the situation.* One or two paragraphs of the concrete thing that started this essay: the scene, the habit, the small failure. Stay specific — a Tuesday, a street, a number.

*Then the turn.* Where the essay stops describing and starts arguing. What did the situation teach you that you didn't expect? This is usually where a weaker draft ends; it's where yours gets going.

*Then the widening.* Connect the small thing to the larger one, carefully. Resist the grand claim — one honest step outward beats three sweeping ones.

*End on the ground, not in the air.* Come back to the scene you opened with, changed by everything in between. Aim for 400–900 words; a letter should be finishable with one cup of something.
