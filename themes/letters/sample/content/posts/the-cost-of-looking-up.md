I read forty-one headlines before breakfast and could not, by noon, remember one. I know the number because I counted, the way you count drinks the morning you first suspect a problem. Forty-one. And when a friend asked at lunch what was going on in the world, I opened my mouth and found the shelf bare.

This is not an essay about the phone. You have read that essay; I have written it twice, both times on the phone. This is about a smaller and more embarrassing discovery: that my attention was not being stolen. It was being spent, by me, in amounts too small to notice, the way a fortune goes missing a few coins at a time.

Here is the test I set myself. For one week, I was allowed to read anything I wanted — but I had to be able to say, out loud, why I was opening it *before* it opened. Not a good reason. Any reason. "I want to know how the vote went." "I love this writer." "I am bored and choosing to be idle." All admissible. The only thing forbidden was the wordless reach.

The first day was mortifying. I caught my thumb moving on its own maybe thirty times, a pianist's hand practicing a scale I never chose. The reasons, when I forced myself to state them, mostly didn't exist. There was no question I was trying to answer. There was only the reach, and behind the reach a faint dread of the unfilled minute.

By the fourth day something older came back. Waiting for the kettle, I looked out the window instead of down, and watched a man across the street fail three times to fold a map — an actual paper map — and laugh at himself, alone, at seven in the morning. I have thought about him more in the weeks since than about anything I read that month. The forty-one headlines cost me nothing each. That is exactly the trouble. Only the week of saying reasons out loud showed me what they cost in total: every man folding a map, every window, every unfilled minute in which a thought might have finished itself.

I don't have a system to sell you. The out-loud rule lapsed, the way rules do. But the reach now comes with a half-second of company — a small voice asking *what are we looking for?* — and half a second, it turns out, is enough. Most of the time there is no answer, and my hand, slightly ashamed of itself, goes back in my pocket.

Attention isn't a resource; that's the language of the people who buy it. It's closer to a location. It is where your life happens, the only place it can. Forty-one times before breakfast, I had been leaving the room my life was in, quietly, without ever saying where I was going.

I am trying, these days, to stay and find out what happens in here.
