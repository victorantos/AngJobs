Every problem I have ever actually solved was solved between my front door and somewhere else. Not at the desk. The desk is where I discover the problem, describe the problem, circle the problem with increasingly ornate notes. The solving happens on foot, usually around the forty-minute mark, usually while I am thinking about something else entirely.

For years I treated this as a charming glitch. Then I read that the body at a walk moves at roughly three miles an hour, and it occurred to me that this is also, more or less, the speed of a thought you can actually follow. The desk offers thinking at zero miles an hour, which curdles into brooding, or at the scroll's velocity, which shears every idea off at the stem. Walking is the one speed at which the mind and the world agree to travel together.

The mechanism, as far as I can observe it from inside, is this: a walk gives the front of the mind a job so undemanding — don't hit the lamppost, nod at the neighbor, notice the dog — that the back of the mind finally gets some privacy. That's where the real work was waiting. The essay that wouldn't end, the conversation I was dreading, the decision I'd been polishing instead of making: the back office handles them all, without appointments, on the sole condition that I stop supervising.

I tested the alternative honestly. One difficult month I decided walks were a luxury and stayed at the desk like a serious person. The work got slower. Not fell apart — nothing so dramatic — just thickened, the way soup left on the heat does. Sentences arrived pre-tired. And I got proportionally better at the desk's own specialty, which is generating reasons why the problem is impossible.

There are rules, learned the slow way. No destination that matters; errands make it a task, and the back office refuses tasks. No phone in hand — pocketed is fine, the way a lifeguard is fine, present and uninvoked. No forcing the question. You do not walk *at* the problem. You walk, and the problem, finding itself unwatched, gets up and quietly rearranges its own furniture. Around minute forty — I have checked this so often it embarrasses me — there is a little click, and something that was a wall becomes a door.

I no longer think of this as a glitch, or even a technique. It is older than that. We thought at a walk for a few hundred thousand years before anyone thought sitting down; the desk is the experiment, three miles an hour the control. When people say they haven't got time for a walk, I recognize the arithmetic, and I know what it misses: the desk hour that the walk replaces was never going to produce anything but a neater description of the wall.

The door is outside. It has always been outside. Go and let yourself be talked out of nothing for forty minutes, and see what's open when you get back.
