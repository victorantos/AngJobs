A letter is written to one person, and that changes every sentence in it. This is the first thing I have to explain when people ask why these weekly essays are called letters, when they go out to a list and not into a single envelope. It isn't nostalgia — or not only. It's a technical decision, and it's the most important one I've made here.

Consider what happens to prose the moment it expects an audience. It straightens its collar. It begins qualifying — *of course, some would argue* — because somewhere in the crowd is the objector, and crowd-prose writes to survive him. It reaches for the general, because the general offends no one in particular. Read anything addressed to everyone and you can hear it: the voice of a person on a stage, palms slightly damp, being careful.

Now read a real letter — your grandmother's, say, or the collected ones of any writer you love, which are so often better than the books. The prose has its jacket off. It says *the* thing, not *a* thing: not "winter can be difficult" but "the pipes froze Tuesday and I cried about nothing at all." A letter can be this specific because it knows exactly who is reading, and knows the reader will extend the one courtesy a crowd never can: they'll assume the best possible meaning, because the letter is *theirs*.

So the form is a trick I play on myself. Each week I write to one reader — an actual person, though I rotate who, and will never tell. The address does the work publication would undo. It bans the survey of the field, the hedge, the throat-clearing paragraph that stands in front of every essay like a doorman. You cannot clear your throat at a friend for two hundred words. They'd put the page down and ask if you were all right.

And something happens on your side of the trick, if I've done it honestly: the letter reads as if it were to you, because in the only sense that matters, it was. Prose aimed at one person arrives personally for each; prose aimed at everyone arrives for no one. This is the paradox the crowd-writers have backwards. The way to reach ten thousand readers is to write, with your whole attention, to one.

The old post understood all this in its bones — the sealed envelope, the single name, the week of silence between question and answer, long enough for a thought to finish becoming itself. I can't ship you the envelope. But the week I can keep, and the single name, and the seal's promise, which was never really about secrecy: *this was meant for you, and it wasn't opened along the way.*

That's the contract. One letter a week, written to one of you at a time.

This was the first one. Now you know whose it was.
