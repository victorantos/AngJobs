The book hadn't changed in twenty years. That was the frightening part. Same yellow spine, same coffee ring on page 212 that I made when I was nineteen, same sentences in the same order — and a completely different book inside them. The novel I remembered was about a young man escaping a small town. The novel in my hands, read at thirty-nine, was about everyone he left there, and it was not on his side.

I had always filed rereading under comfort: the literary equivalent of ordering the usual. Nothing risked, nothing gained, a warm bath in known water. One is short of time; the unread books tilt on the shelf like creditors. Under that arithmetic, rereading looks like paying a debt twice.

But the yellow-spined book demonstrated something the arithmetic misses: you cannot read the same book twice, because the instrument has changed. A first reading tells you about the book. A rereading tells you about the distance between the person who read it last time and whoever is holding it now. Page 212, coffee ring and all, turned out to be a surveyor's mark — fixed precisely so that the movement it measured had to be mine.

At nineteen I underlined the hero's speeches. I found my own pencil lines waiting for me like notes from a confident stranger, and I disagreed with nearly every one. The passages that stopped me this time were ones I had no memory of reading at all: the mother at the sink, given half a page and no speeches, whom the author — I now saw — loved best of anyone. She had been standing there for twenty years, patient as print, waiting for me to be old enough to see her.

This is the case for rereading, and it has nothing to do with comfort. New books tell you what the world contains. Reread books tell you what you have come to contain, and they are the only instrument that measures it. Diaries won't do it; a diary is testimony, and we coach the witness. But your reaction to a fixed page is involuntary. The book holds still precisely so you can catch yourself moving.

So I have amended the arithmetic. For every few new books, one goes back through the turnstile — chosen not from the ones I loved, which is nostalgia, but from the ones that marked me, which is different. I read them the way you visit an old teacher: partly for them, mostly to find out who you have turned out to be in front of someone who knew you when.

The unread books can tilt on the shelf a little longer. They will keep; they aren't going anywhere.

Neither, it turns out, was the mother at the sink. It was only ever me who was moving.
