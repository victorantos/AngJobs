The best line in my notebook is one I didn't write. A woman on the 41 bus, to her friend, about a man neither of them liked: "He's the kind of person who returns a borrowed umbrella wet." I have carried that sentence for six years. It has paid its rent many times over.

People assume a writer's notebook is full of the writer. Mine is mostly other people: overheard verdicts, shop signs, a child's theology ("God can see you but only if you're outside"), the exact wrong flowers at a funeral. My own entries are the weakest things in it — earnest, foggy, trying too hard to sound like entries. The overheard lines are perfect because nobody was trying. They were just talking, and the language, unwatched, did what language does when it thinks no one official is around.

This is why I say a notebook is a net and not a mirror. A mirror shows you yourself, which you already have. A net catches what was passing through anyway — what would otherwise flick its tail once and be gone. The bus line survived because I wrote it down within the minute. I know this because of all its dead siblings: the hundreds of times I thought *that's wonderful, I'll remember that,* and did not. The belief that you will remember is the great extinction event of good sentences.

The discipline, then, is not literary at all. It is closer to birdwatching. You need the notebook *on you*, not in the good bag at home; a pen that works in the cold; the willingness to look slightly odd, stopped on a corner, writing seven words while the light changes. And you need the amnesty rule: nothing in the notebook has to be good. The net is not the meal. Sorting comes later, at the desk, where you find that nine entries in ten are nothing — a phrase that died overnight, a mystery ("green door, ask about the dog??") you can no longer solve. Those are the cost of the tenth.

What changed me wasn't the keeping; it was the rereading. Once a season I go back through, and the notebook turns out to be a record of what I was *noticing*, which is a truer diary than any diary I've managed. The winter I was unhappy, every entry is about closed shutters and people eating alone — I hadn't told the notebook I was unhappy, but the net doesn't lie about where it was cast.

Six years on, I still don't know who the umbrella man was. But I know the woman on the 41 was a better writer than I am, for one sentence, once, for free. The notebook's whole argument is that the world is full of such people, saying such things, all day long, to no one in particular.

Somebody should be catching them. It may as well be you.
