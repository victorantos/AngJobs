I keep a list of everything I've ever quit, and it is the most encouraging document I own. The piano. A law degree, eighteen months in. A novel at page ninety. A friendship that ran on apologies. A city. Two jobs whose names I say the way you name old storms. Thirty-one entries as of this winter, each with a date, kept in the back of a notebook like the ledger it is.

I started it as penance, honestly. Our whole vocabulary for stopping is criminal: you *abandon* a project, *walk out on* a job, *give up* on a person. Quitters never win. I wanted a record of my crimes, I think, some winter I was feeling sorry for myself. It refused to read that way. Laid out in a column with dates, the quittings looked less like a rap sheet and more like a route — the visible half of every choice I was glad I'd made. I could not find the entry I regretted.

Take the law degree, my most expensive quit. Eighteen months of sunk cost, and sunk cost was exactly the argument for staying: *you've come so far.* Notice that this is never an argument about the road ahead — only about the road behind, which is spent identically whether you stop or continue. The day I finally saw that, I left, and the relief was so physical I remember the weather. What I actually lost by quitting was the eighteen months. What I'd have lost by not quitting was all the rest of them.

The ledger taught me the pattern I now watch for. Every entry, without exception, was late. I have never once quit too early — my errors run entirely the other way, one to four years of knowing, quietly, underneath the trying. The knowing arrives long before the permission. What the list measures, entry by entry, is the gap between the two, and the project of my forties is to shorten it.

There's a page I hold against the ledger: the things I didn't quit — this weekly letter among them, which has wanted quitting many times. The two lists rhyme. What the kept things have in common is that staying was a decision, renewed in the open, on the merits. What the quit things have in common is that staying had become a *default*, enforced by embarrassment. That's the whole diagnostic, I think. Persistence and entrapment can look identical from outside; from inside, the question is who's doing the deciding.

So this is not a letter in praise of quitting everything. It is a letter against the crooked bookkeeping that counts every ending as a loss and no staying as a cost. Keep the list; that's my actual advice. Write down what you stopped and when. If you're anything like me, you'll find no regrets in the stopping column at all —

only, here and there, a note that reads: *should have been sooner.*
