Two years ago I committed to publishing twice a month. Forty-one posts later,
here's the audit.

## The numbers

- **Traffic:** most posts get a few hundred readers. One — the Postgres queue
  post — got two hundred thousand, entirely because of an aggregator I don't
  control. The distribution is a power law and you don't get to pick the
  exponent.
- **Revenue:** three Copperline customers have told me directly that a post is
  why they signed up. Three customers over two years sounds bad until you do
  the lifetime math, at which point the blog out-earns most of my marketing.
- **Time:** four to six hours a post. The queue post took fourteen. Writing is
  not free; it competes with shipping, and some months shipping loses.

## What the numbers miss

The private returns turned out to be the real ones.

**Writing is a debugger for thinking.** Half my posts started as "this is
obviously true" and fell apart in the second draft. The
architecture posts especially: explaining a system to a reader finds the part
you were hand-waving to yourself. Twice I've changed the actual system while
writing about it, because the prose made the flaw undeniable.

**The archive answers email.** Every recurring question — how the queue works,
why I don't take investment, what I'd migrate first — is now a link. A public
logbook is a support system with permalinks.

**It compounds identity, not audience.** After two years, the people who show
up are the ones who like how I think. That's a filter working in both
directions, and it's worth more than a big number of the wrong readers.

## The only advice

Lower the bar and raise the frequency. The viral post cannot be planned; the
forty-one-post archive can. Write the logbook. The flukes land on top of it.
