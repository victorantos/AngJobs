The Tidemill occupies three listed buildings around a working harbour, joined by a courtyard, a footbridge, and one genuinely confusing staircase. Guests loved the place and could not find their rooms in it.

## The challenge

Heritage rules meant nothing could be fixed to the external stonework, and the owners refused — rightly — to fill a 300-year-old interior with backlit acrylic. The signage had to guide first-time guests at night, in rain, with luggage, while remaining almost invisible to anyone not looking for it.

## The approach

We mapped every journey a guest actually makes — arrival, room, breakfast, checkout — and found that only nine decisions needed signing. Each got a single oak fingerpost or painted door lintel, using a three-band colour language taken from the harbour itself: navy for the Quay House, foam for the Mill, sand for the Cottages. Room numbers are hand-painted at lantern height, where eyes actually fall in the dark.

![Directional lintel painting in the three-band harbour palette](/media/project-tidemill-detail.svg)

## The outcome

Night-porter callouts for lost guests dropped from a nightly event to roughly one a week. The signage passed conservation review without amendment, and the hotel now sells a small letterpress map of the three buildings at reception — the same drawing we made for the survey, which guests kept trying to take home.
