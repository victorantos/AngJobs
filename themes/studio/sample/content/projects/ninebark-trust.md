The Ninebark River Trust restores chalk streams. Their annual report goes to funders, councils, and four hundred volunteers — and for years it had been a stapled PDF of tables that nobody opened twice.

## The challenge

The trust's data is genuinely good news — fish counts rising, phosphate levels falling — but it was invisible inside spreadsheet exports. The brief was to make one document that works as a funder's evidence pack and a volunteer's keepsake, on a print budget of one colour plus black.

## The approach

We let the river structure the book. A single meander line runs through every spread — it is the actual course of the Ninebark, drawn from the trust's own survey data — and each chapter opens where a restoration site sits on that line. Charts are stripped to one comparison each: this year against last year, drawn at a size you can read across a village-hall table. The second colour is a river green used only for water and for good news.

![Spread from the report showing the meander line and a single-comparison chart](/media/project-ninebark-detail.svg)

## The outcome

The report helped secure a three-year funding renewal, and the trust ran out of its first print run in a month — volunteers were taking extra copies for neighbours. The meander device now appears on the trust's site signage, drawn from the same survey file.
