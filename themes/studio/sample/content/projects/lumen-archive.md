When a local photography society dissolved, its archive — four thousand street photographs from the 1950s to the 1970s, most never printed — nearly went to a skip. A volunteer group scanned everything in eighteen months, then asked us how anyone would ever look at it.

## The challenge

Four thousand images is not an exhibition, it is a warehouse. The site had to make the archive browsable without flattening it into an infinite scroll, work on the parish-council budget of nearly nothing, and put the photographs first — the volunteers were adamant that no interface should ever be more interesting than a picture.

## The approach

We designed the site as a sequence of small, curated walls: twelve photographs at a time, hung edge to edge, selected by theme — market days, weather, children, work. Every photograph opens full-bleed with only a caption in the corner, set like the pencil note on the back of a print. The archive's whole interface is monochrome; the only colour on any page arrives with the photographs themselves.

![Interface frames from the archive, twelve images hung as a wall](/media/project-lumen-detail.svg)

## The outcome

The site launched with eight walls and now grows by one each month, chosen by a rotating volunteer curator. It drew 40,000 visitors in its first quarter, three regional-press features, and — the metric the volunteers cared about — 212 emails identifying people, streets, and shops in the photographs.
