The regional rail company informed the town this week, with the pride of an
organization announcing a bypass, that the station clock will be
synchronized to network time during July's platform works. It is currently
four minutes fast. It has been four minutes fast since 1962.

We would like to register, on behalf of every generation that has run for
the 7:40 believing it was 7:44, our complete opposition.

The clock's error is not a malfunction; it is a public service with sixty
years of uptime. Everyone in Westhaven knows the clock is fast. Visitors
learn within a day, from a local delighted to tell them. The result is a
town that arrives on time by way of a shared, gentle fiction — a municipal
grace period, hand-wound weekly by whichever stationmaster inherits the
duty.

Synchronized time is available on every phone in every pocket. What is not
available on any phone is the experience of trusting your neighbors'
arithmetic instead.

The rail company says the correction is "part of a standard accessibility
and accuracy programme." We say: paint the footbridge, fix the tannoy, and
leave the clock's four minutes where six decades of commuters put them.

Fast clocks make punctual towns. Correct ones just make lonely platforms.
