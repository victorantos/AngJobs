WESTHAVEN — The first commercial oyster harvest since 1966 came ashore last
Tuesday: 4,000 natives, lifted from the restored beds off Pelham Reach by
the eight-member estuary co-operative.

The catch is commercially modest — it sold out to three restaurants by
Thursday — but the co-op's founders always described the oysters as a
by-product. Each adult oyster filters up to two hundred litres of water a
day, and June's estuary readings were the cleanest since monitoring began
in 1978.

The original beds died in the 1960s from a combination of overharvesting
and the tannery outfall, which closed in 1981. Restoration began in 2019
with 40,000 juveniles raised in tanks behind the harbormaster's office.

"Sixty years of people saying the estuary's finished," said co-op chair
Bridie Nolan, whose grandfather worked the old beds. "It wasn't finished.
It was waiting."

The co-op will lift no more stock until autumn, and caps the annual harvest
at a tenth of the population. The restaurants have asked for more. The
oysters, Nolan says, have not been consulted and would decline.
